Q9.1: How do you manage slippages? If it crosses your SL and it doesn't get filled, do you sell at market price or wait for it to come back to your SL?

**Answer:**

When a stop loss is triggered but not filled due to slippage, I immediately execute at whatever price is available and exit the position. I never wait for the price to come back to my stop loss level.

**Why This Approach:**

1. **Risk Management Priority**: My primary focus is on risk management. I never allow a small loss to turn into a big loss.

2. **Mental Health Protection**: Big losses aren't just monetary - they severely impact mental well-being and can lead to thoughts of quitting trading entirely.

3. **Personal Responsibility**: Every trading decision - entry, stop loss placement, and whether to stay in a losing trade - is your responsibility. The market doesn't tell you to wait; that's your own decision.

**The Danger of Waiting:**

- Markets are not forgiving when you break your own rules
- Waiting might work occasionally, but you can't repeat mistakes 100 times and expect consistent success
- Markets that forgive mistakes (like 2020-2021) create false confidence
- The recent market environment has been particularly unforgiving of rule-breaking

**The Solution: Rules Eliminate Confusion**

Having clear rules solves all trading dilemmas, especially exit rules. Most traders struggle more with exits than entries because:
- Buying is relatively easier to learn
- Exiting requires more discipline and has more complex scenarios
- Rules provide clarity in emotionally charged situations

**Key Takeaway**: Always follow your stop loss rules without exception. The market's behavior is unpredictable, but your discipline should be unwavering.

**Consolidation Quality Analysis:**

Long wicks indicate trapped people waiting for price to come back so they can exit. This creates resistance and rejection at those levels, making it harder for the price to break through.

**Number of Tight Days:**

(d) **More Tight Days â‰  Better Breakout**: This is false. Too much tightness or too much buying force is not normal price action. There needs to be a balance.

- Ideal: 3-4 days (most common in successful setups)
- Maximum: Don't wait too long beyond 3-4 days
